UL 142 vs UL 2085 Fireguard®: Which Tank Do You Actually Need?

A side-by-side comparison of the two most common aboveground fuel storage tank standards — and how to know which one your project requires.

Hughes Tank Company · April 15, 2026 · 6 min read

Few decisions in fuel storage cause more confusion than choosing between a UL 142 tank and a UL 2085 Fireguard® tank. Both are aboveground steel tanks. Both store the same fuels. They look similar from the outside. But the price difference can be substantial — and the wrong choice can mean failing a fire marshal inspection or paying for a tank you didn't need.

Here's the practical breakdown.

The short answer

UL 142 is the basic standard for steel aboveground tanks. It covers construction, welding, materials, and labeling for tanks storing flammable and combustible liquids. Think of it as the floor — every aboveground steel fuel tank in the United States should be UL 142 listed at minimum.

UL 2085 Fireguard® is UL 142 plus a two-hour fire rating. The tank has lightweight insulating concrete between the primary and secondary steel walls, which protects the contents during a sustained fire. It's also ballistic and impact rated.

You need UL 2085 when local code requires fire protection. Otherwise, UL 142 is the right choice.

When does code require UL 2085?

The Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ) — usually your local fire marshal — makes the call. Common situations that trigger UL 2085 requirements:

  • Retail fuel stations within urban or suburban fire codes
  • Tanks placed within property line setbacks that wouldn't otherwise be allowed
  • Sites near occupied structures (schools, hospitals, residential)
  • High-traffic public fueling sites
  • Government and defense fueling installations with security requirements

The benefit of UL 2085 isn't just code compliance — it eliminates the need for bollards, dikes, and large setbacks that a UL 142 tank would require in the same location. For tight retail sites, that often makes UL 2085 the only practical option.

Cost difference

UL 2085 tanks cost roughly 2x to 3x more than equivalent UL 142 tanks of the same capacity. The difference comes from:

  • The insulating concrete pour between walls
  • Heavier secondary shell construction
  • UL 2085 listing and labeling fees
  • Heavier shipping weight (think 2-3x heavier than the same capacity in UL 142)

You can also factor in installation costs. A UL 142 tank may need bollards, a containment dike, and larger setbacks — costs that disappear with UL 2085's integrated protection.

Capacity and configuration

Both standards cover the full range Hughes Tank manufactures: 300 to 25,000 gallons in single-wall, double-wall, cylindrical, and rectangular configurations. UL 2085 is also available in compartmented designs for storing multiple products in one tank.

What about Flameshield®?

Flameshield® is the third option. It's an STI-listed alternative to UL 2085 that uses an intumescent coating between the primary and secondary steel walls instead of concrete. It delivers the same two-hour fire performance but at a lighter weight and lower cost than UL 2085. Some AHJs accept Flameshield® where UL 2085 would otherwise be required — confirm with your fire marshal before you specify.

How to decide

The decision flows in this order:

  1. Ask your AHJ first. Your local fire marshal will tell you whether fire-rated storage is required at your specific site. Don't guess.
  2. If no fire rating is required → UL 142. It's the standard, it's UL-listed, it's compliant, and it costs less.
  3. If fire rating is required → UL 2085 Fireguard® or Flameshield®. Flameshield® is lighter and cheaper; UL 2085 is the most universally accepted listing.
  4. If the site is tight or near occupied structures → Even if not strictly required, fire-rated tanks eliminate setbacks and bollards that may otherwise consume your site.
