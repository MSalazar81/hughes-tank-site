Specifying Fuel Storage for Your Fleet: 7 Considerations Before You Buy

Choosing the right aboveground fuel tank for fleet operations involves more than capacity. Here's what experienced fleet managers think about before they specify.

Hughes Tank Company · April 01, 2026 · 5 min read

A fleet fueling tank is a long-term capital asset. Get the spec right and you have a 20-year answer to your fueling problem. Get it wrong and you're paying to replace it sooner — or operating around limitations every day. Here are the seven decisions that matter most.

1. Capacity — and not just the number you first think of

The instinct is to size the tank to "average weekly fuel use times two." That works as a starting point, but think about:

  • Delivery economics. Fuel suppliers typically discount for full tanker loads (usually 8,000-10,000 gallons in one drop). If your tank can only hold 4,000 gallons, you're paying for partial loads for life.
  • Reserve for emergencies. Hurricane season, winter storms, supply disruptions — at the moment you most need fuel, you don't want to be at 10%.
  • Future fleet growth. Sizing for today's fleet is sizing for under-capacity in 5 years.

The general rule we see: size for 1.5x your peak monthly burn rate, with capacity to receive a full tanker drop without going under 25%.

2. Aboveground or underground?

Aboveground is usually the right answer for fleet operations:

  • Lower installed cost (no excavation, no concrete vault)
  • Easier inspection and maintenance access
  • No Tier I/Tier II EPA monitoring requirements
  • Can be relocated if your facility moves

Underground makes sense when site constraints (parking lot space, aesthetics, urban locations) require it, or when local code prohibits aboveground.

3. Single-wall vs double-wall

For any new installation, double-wall is the practical answer. Most jurisdictions require secondary containment, and a double-wall tank with built-in containment is cheaper and cleaner than a single-wall tank with a separate dike. The interstitial space between walls also gives you a clear leak detection point.

Single-wall tanks still have a place — replacement-in-kind for older systems, agricultural use where containment isn't required, and some specialized applications.

4. Dispensing — pump-on-tank or remote dispenser

Two options for getting fuel from the tank to the vehicle:

  • Pump-on-tank. Pump and dispenser mounted on or next to the tank. Vehicles drive up to the tank to fuel. Simple, lower cost, fewer points of failure.
  • Remote dispenser. Tank in one location, fuel piped to dispenser islands elsewhere. More flexible site layout but adds cost and complexity (piping, leak detection on the lines, spill containment at the dispenser).

For most fleets with fewer than 50 vehicles fueling at one location, pump-on-tank is the right call.

5. Monitoring and reporting

Modern tank monitoring is a small investment that pays back fast:

  • Automatic tank gauging (ATG) tells you exactly how much fuel you have, when you got each delivery, and how much each vehicle took.
  • Leak detection in the interstitial space alerts you immediately if the primary shell ever loses integrity.
  • Cellular reporting means your fuel manager and your supplier can see levels remotely — automatic re-orders before you hit empty.

For SPCC-regulated facilities, monitoring isn't optional — it's how you prove compliance.

6. Local code and your AHJ

We covered this in our code basics post, but for fleet specifically:

  • Talk to your local fire marshal before specifying. Some jurisdictions require UL 2085 Fireguard® for fleet locations near property lines or occupied buildings.
  • Check setback requirements. NFPA 30 setbacks for a 10,000-gallon UL 142 tank are different than for the same capacity in UL 2085.
  • Confirm overfill protection requirements. Most codes require both a high-level alarm and an automatic shutoff.

7. Lead time and supplier relationship

Standard tank lead times have ranged from 6 weeks to 6+ months over the past few years, depending on capacity and the manufacturer's backlog. A few things to ask before you commit:

  • What's the manufacturer's current lead time on the size and configuration you need?
  • Will you get CAD drawings before fabrication starts?
  • Does the manufacturer ship direct or through a distributor network?
  • What's the warranty — and is it enforceable through the manufacturer or through the distributor?
  • Who handles delivery freight, and is the freight quoted firm or estimated?
