Fuel Storage Tank Code Basics: NFPA 30, IFC, and EPA Requirements

A plain-English overview of the major codes that govern aboveground and underground fuel storage tanks in the United States.

Hughes Tank Company · April 08, 2026 · 8 min read

Anyone planning a new fuel storage installation runs into the alphabet soup quickly: UL, NFPA, IFC, EPA, SPCC, STI. The codes overlap, reference each other, and sometimes contradict. Here's a clear overview of what each one does and where it applies.

The big four

For most projects in the United States, four bodies of code govern fuel storage:

  • NFPA 30 — The Flammable and Combustible Liquids Code. This is the foundational document. Most state and local codes adopt it by reference.
  • IFC — The International Fire Code. Adopted in most jurisdictions and aligns closely with NFPA 30 but adds local fire department authority.
  • EPA 40 CFR 112 (SPCC) — The Spill Prevention, Control, and Countermeasure rule. Applies to facilities storing more than 1,320 gallons of oil aboveground.
  • EPA 40 CFR 280 — Underground storage tank (UST) requirements.

Beyond these, your tank must be built to a recognized industry standard — usually UL or STI for steel tanks.

NFPA 30 in plain English

NFPA 30 establishes the rules for storing, handling, and using flammable and combustible liquids. For aboveground storage tanks, it covers:

  • Construction standards. The tank must be built to a recognized standard like UL 142 or UL 2085.
  • Distance requirements. Setbacks from property lines, buildings, and other tanks based on tank size and the liquid stored.
  • Secondary containment. Required for tanks storing certain liquids — either via a double-wall tank or a separate dike.
  • Venting and overfill protection. Specific requirements for normal vents and emergency vents based on tank size.
  • Spill control. Containment for fill and dispenser areas.

NFPA 30 is updated every few years. The 2024 edition is current as of this writing. Your local code may reference an older edition — check before you design.

IFC and your local fire marshal

The IFC adopts most of NFPA 30 but adds the role of the Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ). Your local fire marshal has discretion to require additional protections beyond what NFPA 30 strictly mandates — for example, requiring fire-rated tanks (UL 2085) where NFPA 30 alone might allow a UL 142.

This is why we always tell customers: talk to your AHJ before specifying a tank. The same project in two adjacent counties may have different tank requirements.

SPCC — the federal spill prevention rule

If your facility stores more than 1,320 gallons of oil aboveground (counting all containers 55 gallons or larger), you're subject to EPA's Spill Prevention, Control, and Countermeasure (SPCC) rule. SPCC requires:

  • A written SPCC Plan, certified by a Professional Engineer for facilities over 10,000 gallons
  • Secondary containment for all bulk storage containers
  • Regular tank inspections and integrity testing
  • Employee training on spill response
  • Record-keeping for spills, inspections, and training

SPCC is federal — it applies regardless of state or local rules. Penalties for noncompliance are significant.

EPA 40 CFR 280 — underground tanks

Underground storage tanks are governed separately by EPA 40 CFR 280. This rule covers:

  • Tank construction (must be cathodically protected steel or non-corrodible material)
  • Spill and overfill prevention equipment
  • Release detection (continuous monitoring required for most systems)
  • Operator training and record-keeping

Hughes Tank manufactures two UST options that meet 40 CFR 280: Permatank® (STI-P3 fiberglass-coated steel) and ACT-100-U (cathodically-protected steel with factory-applied fiberglass coating).

UL and STI standards

The construction standards for the tank itself come from two main listing bodies:

  • UL 142 — Steel Aboveground Tanks for Flammable and Combustible Liquids. The baseline.
  • UL 2085 — Protected Aboveground Tanks. Two-hour fire-rated.
  • STI Flameshield® — Fire-protected aboveground tank with intumescent coating.
  • STI-P3 (Permatank®) — FRP-coated steel underground tank.
  • STI ACT-100-U — Cathodically-protected steel UST.

State and local variation

State and local codes can — and do — add requirements on top of federal rules. Texas (where we're located) generally adopts the IFC with state amendments through the Texas Commission on Environmental Quality and the State Fire Marshal's Office. California has its own additional rules under Title 23 and Title 19. Coastal states often have stricter spill prevention rules tied to navigable waters.

Where to start on a new project

  1. Talk to your AHJ. Your local fire marshal sets the project's specific requirements.
  2. Determine if SPCC applies. If you'll store more than 1,320 gallons of oil aboveground, you need a written SPCC plan.
  3. Choose aboveground vs underground. Site constraints, code, and operational needs all play in.
  4. Specify the tank standard. UL 142, UL 2085, Flameshield®, Permatank®, or ACT-100-U.
  5. Plan for secondary containment. Either built into the tank (double-wall) or as a separate dike.
  6. Plan for inspection access and overfill prevention. Required by NFPA 30 and SPCC both.
